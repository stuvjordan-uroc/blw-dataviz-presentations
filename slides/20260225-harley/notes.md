# Elected Politicians Hate Democracy

I want you to begin by considering a hypothesis: _Elected politicians hate democracy_. To be clear, this is a **hypothesis**. We don't know it to be true. It's just an idea that we have good reasons for thinking **might** be true. And it's a fruitful starting place for reasoning about events in all kinds of political systems, both democratic and non-democratic.

The first thing I want you to learn today is an argument for thinking this hypothesis might be true. The argument has three steps.

- First, I'll give you a definition of "democracy".
- Second, I'll give you a definition of "politician".
- Third, I'll we'll use those definitions to imagine politician's attitudes toward democracy.

# Why Politicians Hate Democracy

**Ok, so let's start with a definition of Democracy:**

A political system in which officeholders lose office when most of the persons they govern disapprove of their performance.

Now, this is a little abstract, so let me give you an example. Think about the Mayor of the City of Rochester. The Mayor is an example of an government officeholder -- a person who holds an office ("Mayor") in a government -- the City of Rochester. Now...Is the City of Rochester a democracy? Well, you might think that depends on whether or not people vote to decide who gets to be Mayor. But this definition takes a different perspective: It says that whether the City of Rochester is a democracy really comes down to the terms on which the Mayor can keep his job. If he loses his job when most persons who live in Rochester disapprove of his work, the City of Rochester is a democracy. If instead the mayor can keep his job even when most Rochestarians are unhappy with his work, it's not.

**Now let's introduce our definition of politician**: A politician is a person who seeks political power by winning and holding public office.
